## Executive Summary

This article consolidates knowledge captured across four TTK sessions with Senior Process Engineer Schulz concerning batch **SUP30498**, the pilot-to-commercial scale-up of **Enalapril Tablets** (Enalapril Maleate, ACE Inhibitor, ATC: C09AA) on the **Korsch XL800** rotary tablet press. The sessions span 2026-05-15 to 2026-05-16 and represent a progressive, iterative knowledge capture — each session adding resolution to the same underlying failure event.

The primary failure mode was **intermittent tablet capping and lamination** during commercial compression, emerging approximately 45 minutes into the run. The defect was absent at pilot scale (Korsch XL400) under nominally equivalent recipe parameters. Root cause analysis, while not yet fully closed, converges strongly across all sessions on a **compound failure mechanism**: low-end granule moisture (reduced plasticity) interacting with shortened dwell time at commercial turret speeds, compounded by sub-optimal pre-compression force and an uncharacterised relative humidity contribution. A partially effective interim mitigation was applied during the batch run; a formal pilot study has been initiated to confirm root cause and validate the corrected parameter set. A critical piece of tacit knowledge — an undocumented lower punch penetration depth adjustment by press operator Maria — was recovered via TTK interview and is captured here for the first time in a formal knowledge record.

**ICH Q9 Risk Classification: Moderate.** Product quality impact confirmed (elevated rejection rate); patient safety risk is low (defect detectable in-process); process understanding gap partially resolved; investigation ongoing.

## Formulation-Specific Insights

### Enalapril Maleate — Compression Behaviour Profile

1. **Elastic Recovery Sensitivity:** Enalapril maleate exhibits a well-characterised tendency toward elastic recovery under rapid compression cycles. This is the defining formulation-level risk for this scale-up and must be explicitly flagged in the product risk register. All compression scale-up activities for this API should include a mandatory dwell time sensitivity assessment.

2. **Moisture-Plasticity Coupling:** Granule plasticity in this formulation is directly moisture-dependent. At or below 1.2–1.6% LOD, granules behave increasingly elastically under compaction. The practical consequence is that the lower boundary of an acceptable LOD range at pilot scale is **not transferable** to commercial scale when dwell time is shorter. The effective minimum LOD for commercial compression is higher than for pilot compression — estimated at ≥1.8% based on available evidence, pending pilot study confirmation.

3. **Compound Failure Threshold:** The failure mode appears to require the **co-occurrence** of multiple factors to manifest. Low LOD alone at pilot scale (where dwell time was longer) did not cause capping. Higher turret speed alone (if LOD had been at target 2.0%) may not have caused capping. The interaction of both factors — and the absence of either from the pilot experience base — is precisely why the defect was not predicted. Future risk assessments must model factor interactions, not individual parameter ranges in isolation.

4. **Intermittent Pattern as Diagnostic Signal:** The approximately 45-minute delay before defect onset is a critical formulation-process interaction signature. It rules out a simple setup error and points to time-dependent process drift — most likely press temperature rise, RH increase at the die table, or developing feed frame segregation during the run. This characteristic should be incorporated into troubleshooting decision trees for this product.

5. **LOD Specification Gap:** The validated LOD specification (2.0% target) was designed at pilot scale and does not include a differentiated commercial-scale action limit. This is a known formulation documentation gap requiring formal resolution.

## Operator Tacit Knowledge (Undocumented Findings)

> These findings were recovered exclusively through TTK interview sessions and were **not present in batch records or deviation reports** at the time of knowledge capture. They represent critical process assets that would otherwise have been lost.

### Finding 1 — Lower Punch Penetration Depth Adjustment (Operator Maria)
**What happened:** During the commercial run, press operator Maria independently identified that the lower punch penetration depth had been carried over from the pilot setting without adjustment for the XL800 die geometry and feed frame configuration. She increased penetration depth by **0.2 mm deeper** than the pilot-carry-over value.

**Impact:** Significant improvement in die fill consistency and weight uniformity. Weight CV improved materially as part of the combined intervention package.

**Why it was undocumented:** The adjustment fell within the declared equipment operating range and was therefore not required to be logged under the current batch record protocol.

**Risk:** If not formalised, this knowledge leaves with Maria. The next compression team would start from the incorrect pilot carry-over value.

**Required action:** Formalise +0.2 mm adjusted value as the commercial set-point in the compression procedure and CPP. Retrieve absolute value from press data log. Implement a real-time mechanism to capture any in-process penetration depth changes.

### Finding 2 — Humidity as a Time-Dependent Trigger (Schulz — Floor Observation)
**What happened:** Schulz identified that RH at the commercial site was outside the recommended range during the run and linked this to the mid-run onset of capping (~45 minutes in). His hypothesis is that progressive humidity change at the die table — rather than a static setup condition — drove the defect onset timing.

**Why it was undocumented:** Environmental conditions are typically logged at room level, not at die-table level, and the connection between RH drift and defect timing is an observational inference not captured in standard deviation narratives.

**Required action:** Add RH monitoring at die-table level to the in-process monitoring plan. Retrieve any available room RH logs for the run period and correlate with defect onset timing. Include in pilot study design.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Dwell time is an independent critical process parameter — treat it as such.** Matching compression force (kN) between pilot and commercial equipment does not ensure equivalent consolidation. Effective dwell time — determined by turret diameter, turret speed, and punch head flat length — must be explicitly calculated for every press at every target RPM and compared between sites as a mandatory scale-up deliverable. This gap was the central failure in the SUP30498 transfer.

2. **LOD specifications developed at pilot scale require re-evaluation before commercial compression.** The lower boundary of the validated LOD range may not be achievable at commercial scale when dwell time is materially shorter. For moisture-sensitive formulations such as enalapril maleate, establish a commercial-specific lower action limit (≥1.8% for this product) via formal change control.

3. **Pre-compression force requires fresh optimisation at commercial scale, not proportional transfer.** Press geometry, punch head flat dimensions, turret diameter, and cycle speed all influence early-stage consolidation dynamics independently of kN values transferred from the pilot.

4. **Intermittent, mid-run defects signal process drift, not setup errors.** Standard troubleshooting focused on initial setup parameters will miss time-dependent causes such as RH rise at the die table, press temperature build-up, or feed frame segregation. Monitoring protocols must include time-resolved in-process checks specifically designed to detect mid-run drift.

5. **Operator tacit knowledge is a quality system asset and a documentation liability simultaneously.** The penetration depth intervention by operator Maria provided measurable, quantified process improvement. That it was not captured in any formal record represents a systemic gap in knowledge management — not an individual failing. The responsibility for closing that gap lies with the quality management system, not the operator.

6. **Punch position clustering of defects is diagnostic, not merely operational.** When specific punch positions present worst-case defect rates and tooling is ruled out, the signal should prompt investigation of turret dynamics, feed frame geometry at those angular positions, and local temperature or humidity gradients. This diagnostic logic should be embedded in compression troubleshooting SOPs.

7. **Scale-up risk assessments must model factor interactions, not individual parameter tolerances.** Neither low LOD alone nor high turret speed alone caused this failure at the levels observed. The risk assessment approach of evaluating parameters independently would have missed this event even with complete individual parameter data from the pilot.

8. **Knowledge capture sessions at deviation close-out are not optional.** Multiple critical findings in this case (RH drift hypothesis, moisture lever insight, penetration depth adjustment, defect onset timing) exist only because a structured TTK session was conducted. Without it, the next campaign team would have started from an incomplete and partially incorrect knowledge base.

## Recommendations for Future Scale-Ups

### Mandatory Pre-Campaign Activities

**R1 — Dwell Time Calculation (Mandatory Deliverable)**
Calculate effective dwell time for the commercial press at all planned turret speeds before the first commercial batch. Document punch head flat dimensions, turret diameter, and RPM range for both pilot and commercial equipment. Compare calculated dwell times; if commercial dwell time is more than 15–20% shorter than pilot, treat the difference as a risk trigger requiring proactive process adjustment. Include dwell time profile in the scale-up risk assessment and tech transfer package as a controlled document.

**R2 — LOD Commercial Action Limit (Change Control Required)**
Establish a commercial-specific lower LOD action limit of **≥1.8%** for Enalapril Tablets, pending confirmation from the initiated pilot study. Blends arriving below 1.8% LOD must not proceed to compression without a formal process engineering review and documented risk acceptance. Implement via change control to the blend specification and commercial BMR.

**R3 — Pre-Compression Force Re-Optimisation Protocol**
Conduct a formal pre-compression force optimisation (minimum 3-point force bracketing at target turret speed) during scale-up validation before the first full commercial run. Do not carry over pilot pre-compression values numerically. Recommended commercial starting range for XL800: 4.0–5.0 kN (to be confirmed by pilot study).

**R4 — Punch Penetration Depth Formalisation**
Include lower punch penetration depth as an explicitly named, specified, and controlled parameter in the commercial compression procedure (CPP and equipment setup sheet). Set the commercial set-point at the confirmed corrected value (pilot value +0.2 mm; retrieve absolute value from XL800 press data log). Remove any implicit or default carry-over from pilot documentation.

**R5 — Commercial Turret Speed Upper Limit**
Define a formulation-specific maximum turret speed for Enalapril Tablets based on minimum acceptable dwell time, not on throughput targets. Production rate targets must not drive turret speed above the dwell-time-derived ceiling without a formal process change assessment and re-validation.

### Mandatory In-Process Monitoring Additions

**R6 — RH Monitoring at Die-Table Level**
Add relative humidity monitoring at die-table level (not room level) as a mandatory in-process monitoring parameter for all commercial OSD compression runs at this site. Define alert (e.g., >50% RH) and action limits (e.g., >55% RH) appropriate to the formulation. Include RH trend data in deviation investigations for any mid-run defect onset.

**R7 — Structured Mid-Run Capping Check Window**
Insert a formal in-process monitoring check at **30–60 minutes** into every commercial compression run for products being compressed at commercial scale for the first time. This check should specifically assess capping and lamination incidence, not just weight and hardness. Capping emerging mid-run (not at startup) is an under-monitored failure mode not captured by standard start-up checks.

**R8 — Real-Time Weight CV Monitoring with Defined Action Trigger**
Monitor weight CV in real time during commercial campaigns. A CV exceeding **1.2%** should trigger immediate investigation of feed frame speed and penetration depth before tablet rejection rates escalate. Define this formally as an in-process action limit in the BMR.

### Systemic / Knowledge Management Actions

**R9 — Dwell Time Calculation Template (Standard Tool)**
Develop a standardised dwell time calculation template for the OSD compression team, outputting effective dwell time for a given press model, die diameter, and turret speed, and comparing it against formulation-specific minimum dwell time requirements. This template should be a mandatory input to every compression scale-up risk assessment.

**R10 — Real-Time In-Process Adjustment Capture Mechanism**
Implement a structured mechanism for capturing operator in-process adjustments in real time — for example, a structured press log, a dedicated field in the electronic batch record system, or an HMI-level prompt — that records any manual parameter adjustment with rationale, regardless of whether the adjustment is within the declared operating range. Adjustments within range are as information-rich as out-of-range deviations.

**R11 — Post-Run Operator Debrief (Structured, Minimum 15 Minutes)**
Implement a mandatory structured operator debrief at the close of any compression run involving a deviation, parameter adjustment, or elevated rejection rate. This debrief should specifically seek observations not reflected in automated data logs: timing of defect onset, visual patterns, environmental observations, and adjustment rationale. Output should be a dated, signed knowledge capture record retained with the batch deviation file.

**R12 — Formulation Risk Register — Elastic Recovery Flag**
Enalapril maleate's elastic recovery tendency should be explicitly listed in the product formulation risk register, flagged as a trigger for mandatory dwell time evaluation at every compression scale-up or press change. This flag should also trigger compaction simulation or Heckel analysis as part of any future scale-up package for this formulation.

**R13 — Pilot Study Close-Out and Article Update**
The formal pilot study initiated following SUP30498 must be closed out and its confirmed findings used to update this Knowledge Article before the next commercial batch of this product is released to compression. Specifically: confirm the validated commercial LOD range, validate the corrected turret speed and pre-compression force set-points, and formally resolve the RH contribution to defect onset timing.
